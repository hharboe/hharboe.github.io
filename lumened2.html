LUMENED: CONNECTING CLASSROOMS

LumenEd was the education technology startup I co-founded my junior year at Oberlin College, and worked on full-time post-graduation. We designed and shipped a hardware, software, and curriculum product package to K-8 classrooms. For every package sold, we donated the equivalent to an underprivileged classroom in India.

MY ROLE

I was one of three co-founders, and the design, sales, and marketing lead. In addition to many joyful tasks as co-founder, my responsibilities included curriculum and software design, sales to K-8 schools, and designing our web, print, and video materials.

My two co-founders, Prakash Paudel, and Thomas Kreek, lead software development, fundraising, and hardware development.

OVERVIEW

THREE YEARS OF EDTECH

LumenEd began on stage at a pitch competition in Ohio, where three college juniors held a cardboard box containing a projector connected to a Raspberry Pi. Three years later, we had shipped thirty units of The Bright Orange Box: a battery-powered smart projector designed for offline classrooms, and were re-enrolling classrooms across five countries for Video Pen Pals: a global education program for K-8 classrooms.

Along the way, some highlights included:

  • Selling $45k in classroom subscriptions in our first three months of sales
  • Raising $90k in grants, Kickstarter, and pre-seed investment
  • Hiring a team of 5 educators in our New Delhi office
  • Deploying 30 custom projectors to Indian classrooms
  • Being a Keynote speaker at the 2015 online Global Education Conference

RESEARCH

WHY A PROJECTOR IN A CLASSROOM?

LumenEd's inspiration came in 2013 from the growing culture of video-based education in the US, and the pervasive inaccessibility of online videos in underprivileged parts of the world. Our question was simple: how would offline classrooms take advantage of a media player, video camera, and internet connection?

Using the latest microprocessors, LED-based projectors, and video cameras of 2013, we saw an opportunity to create a robust classroom hub for video creation and consumption. Our vision was to create an empowered network of disparate classrooms, discovering new subject matter and sharing their unique perspectives with the rest of the world.

PILOTING THE CONCEPT

In summer 2013, one of our co-founders brought a retail projector, preloaded with educational videos, to a few underresourced classrooms in New Delhi. We heard lots of enthusiasm for the concept from teachers and students.

With motivation to test a custom smart projector the following summer, we observed and documented technical requirements for our hardware.

PILOT FINDINGS: HARDWARE REQUIREMENTS

  • Portable
    Projectors should be portable, so that teachers can share them between classrooms, and take them home at night
  • Battery-powered
    Many classrooms have no plug points, and power outages are common
  • 3G-connectivity
    There is no wifi access in most classrooms, but widespread 2G and 3G network access
  • Extra bright
    Many classrooms are lit by daylight without shades, making dim projectors hard to see
  • Extra loud
    Speakers must be loud enough to play over ambient chatter from 30-50 students and typical background noises from a school
  • Simple interface
    Many students and teachers are unfamiliar with projectors and media players

ITERATING IN THE FIELD

In winter 2014, we received $10k from social impact grants and a startup pitch competition that funded our travel and development costs. We set out to test two applications of our hardware: playing video content in Indian classrooms, and facilitating a video exchange program between classrooms.

CONCLUSION 1: HARDWARE IS... HARD

From the end of spring into summer 2014, much of our time was devoted to the design, assembly, and shipment of our BOB prototypes. We ran into numerous headaches throughout this process, teaching us that pursuing BOBs in the future would be best accomplished through a professional manufacturer.

Teachers' feedback on the BOB was positive, but we knew we couldn't sell the BOB directly to schools. Selling to the government would be a large undertaking, so we were in search for a sustainable hardware business model.

CONCLUSION 2: TEACHERS PREFER THEIR OWN CONTENT

We were unsure if teachers would be capable or motivated to find their own educational videos, so we made an attempt to pre-loading BOBs with a library of videos mapped to Indian educational standards.

We curated a library of open-source videos, and built a simple app interface teachers could use to browse content. Teachers appreciated the gesture, but we learned that they ultimately prefer finding and playing their own materials.

CONCLUSION 3: VIDEO EXCHANGES ARE A SUCCESS

We found three schools in the US willing to participate in a video exchange project with our Indian school partners. We helped these classrooms send video diaries to each other over three months, covering their lives at school, their local communities, and favorite cultural practices.

The concept was a success. Students on both sides of the exchange had wonderful experiences filming videos in their classrooms, and seeing videos from their new friends abroad. We learned that Indian classrooms needed hardware to participate, and US classrooms were enthusiastic enough to pay for the experience again.
