Michael: Cycling Assistant

In November 2017, an electric bike startup hired me to design a prototype for their new chatbot. That December, I started a 4 month contract to build a Beta version, in collaboration with one developer and our client.

My work included:

  • Designing a visual identity and prototype
  • Leading generative research to refine our focus
  • Making a design language for copy and visuals
  • Conducting usability tests to optimize UI
  • Using gamification to boost engagement

Sections

SCOPING

PROTOTYPING THE CONCEPT

In fall 2017, Faraday Bicycles Inc. wanted to build a chatbot to integrate with their electric bikes. I was contracted to refine their initial concept, and design a visual prototype.

Requests

  • Design a chatbot character who is entertaining and brand-aligned
  • Mockup interface components for customer support, and activity data visualizations

Results

Faraday opted to pass the project, but one employee, Thomas Kreek, decided to continue on his own. He offered me and a developer contracts to build a functional Beta version.

SCOPING THE BETA

Refining goals

Our contract's initial goals felt ambitious, so I suggested we narrow our focus, and success metrics, with a "NUF" brainstorm.

  • Educate: Teach non-cylists to become capable riders
  • Engage: Help frequent cyclists plan their rides
  • Mobilize: Create a brand for cycling evangelists to rally behind

Defining success metrics

Despite the feasibility risks, our client chose to focus on Motivation, with a goal of helping users increase their cycling habits.

To track progress, we would focus on days per week cycled, from 0–4 weeks after sign up. We would also ask users to periodically rate their cycling confidence.

RESEARCH

GENERATIVE RESEARCH

My research included a literature review and user interviews. My literature review had two objectives:

Objectives

  • Identify problem(s) to focus on
  • Refine demographic of target cyclists/non-cyclists

Identifying problems

I read studies on new and irregular cyclists' behavior, and mapped common deterrents into three categories. I crossed-out problems that looked unsolvable by software.

Location

  • Hilly terrains
  • Dense traffic
  • Routes over 10km

Lifestyle

  • Lack of time
  • Low confidence*
  • Few opportunities*

Equipment

  • No bike*
  • Broken bike*
  • Safety equipment*

Based on the literature, the most prevalent problems were out of our scope. Many solvable problems had existing solutions, including cycling education classes, and shops that fix bicycle equipment.

I hoped to uncover why existing solutions weren't being utilized—perhaps many people face confounding deterrents, or local solutions are inaccessible.

Understanding demographics

Many studies categorize cyclists based on psychological qualities. One convincing Portland-based study built a typology of cyclists based on rider confidence and enthusiasm.

Using their study as an analogy for our target users, we decided to focus on the groups in blue, who lack the confidence or enthusiasm to adopt a regular cycling routine.

Applying a behavioral change model

With this focus on boosting confidence and enthusiasm, we found inspiration in a behavioral change model popularized by Stanford’s Persuasive Technology Lab.

This simple model became the foundation for our chatbot's educative process. We hoped our interviews would identify the elements of Ability, Motivation, and Triggers prioritized by our target users.

CYCLIST INTERVIEWS

Objectives

  • Understand interactions between solvable/unsolvable problems
  • Identify crucial tasks in journeys to develop cycling routines
  • Distinguish behavioral differences between rider types

Asking open-ended questions

We spoke with 16 adults across a range of demographics, cities, and cycling aptitudes. Our discussion addressed past cycling experiences, and habits related to maintaining cycling routines.

Topic 1: Location

"Have you biked in your current neighborhood? Why or why not?"
"When and where have you ever biked for transportation?"

Topic 2: Lifestyle

"How would cycling add or detract from your daily routine?"
"What do you enjoy most about biking?"

Topic 3: Equipment

"How has your bicycle’s condition kept you from cycling in the past?"
"How would you describe your local bicycle shop?"

Identifying a niche audience

Our interviews showed that our target demographic was smaller than expected. Their most common deterrents included traffic fears, weather discomfort, and bike maintainance.

I constructed user personas to align our team on who and who not to design for. Having names and faces to point to was very helpful during the design process.

Focusing on crucial tasks

We used interviews to identify and categorize tasks that are crucial to developing cycling routines. The goal of our chatbot would be to make these tasks more approachable for our target demographic.

DESIGN

CHOOSING TECHNOLOGY

Goals

  • Choose our chatbot's development and distribution platform
  • Identify chosen platform's affordances and constraints

Comparing affordances

We decided to release on Facebook Messenger, rather than a native app, or different chatbot platform. Messenger would be the quickest to bootstrap, and its popularity and affordances were attractive.

Documenting constraints

Before designing, I documented the constraints we faced by using Messenger's conversational features and UI:

  • Character limits and layout restrictions of card templates
  • Absence of traditional navigation affordances
  • Brevity required for a chat-based, mobile UI

CHARACTER DESIGN

Objectives

  • Create guidelines for writing cohesive, character-driven copy
  • Design flexible templates for all visual content

Creating a personality

My client wanted to add emotional weight to our user interactions with meaningful, character-driven copy. I wrote copywriting guidelines, describing Michael as, "a curious scientist, and caring fitness coach, with high expectations."

1. Humanize Michael

  • Write in familiar language
  • Avoid monotony, excessive '!’s
  • Use facts, not platitudes
“Everyone can ride in the summer!”
"It is a perfect 70° today. I recommend cycling in a t-shirt.”

2. Build user confidence

  • Treat users with confidence
  • Ask users to one-up their best
  • Avoid vapid praise
“Great job! You are doing amazing.”
“You biked 5 miles today. How about 7 tomorrow?”

Optimizing for mobile

To display visual information, I designed a simple, flexible card template. Knowledge Cards were the first template application, using bite-size information to guide users through crucial cycling tasks.

ITERATIONS ON UI

Objectives

  • Minimize friction of conversation-based navigation
  • Fit information architecture to users' mental models
  • Prototype and test for unknown frictions in chatbot interface

Documenting frictions

We built a two-level navigation system, where users could swipe through parent topics, before exploring detailed Knowledge Cards.

Early usability testing showed non-cyclists feeling intimidated to explore cards by topic. I compiled feedback into two problems:

1. Lack of Substance

  • Knowledge Cards alone did not create a lasting effect on users’ confidence.
“This is hard for me. To be honest, I’m not sure what to learn about.”

2. Poor navigation

  • Our navigation structure did not match users’ mental models for learning
“I can see this being helpful, but I think it’s too tedious for me."

Matching real-life scenarios

To give users more context, we combined multiple Knowledge Cards and API calls to create guidance around common cycling scenarios.

Supporting Q&A interactions

We observed most users starting their chatbot interaction by asking a question. To fit these users' mental models, we trained a NLP model to interpret user questions and direct them to the most relevant card.

GAMIFYING FEATURES

Throughout testing, users questioned the desirability of our features. Users called our cycling guide "uninteresting" and "incomplete." When asked if our chatbot was empowering towards fitness or commute goals, some intermediate cyclists said, "not at all."

Our team decided to "gamify" our chatbot. This meant incorporating our existing features into activity- or education-based challenges, that users could progress through via chatbot interactions.

Collecting feedback

In a survey collecting feedback on game ideas, 25% of inexperienced riders, and 0% of experienced riders expressed any interest. One rider said, "I'm very happy already with Strava, where I can set my own goals. I don't need someone inventing arbitrary tasks for me."

Despite this negative response, my client asked us to continue to develop three cycling games in the last two months of our contract.

Designing around user goals

I wrote each game's logic and copy around users' cycling goals, and steps we recommended to achieve them. Our games included:

  • Zero to One: Transitioning from non-cyclist to regular cyclist
  • Marco Polo: Biking to more local destinations
  • Workplace Warrior: Starting a bike-to-work commute routine

Improving usability

Usability testing revealed users were frustrated by "walls of text," and didn't know "how far [they had] progressed" in each challenge. To address this, I added more frequent checkpoints, emojis to break-up text, and image cards for richer displays of data.

We also made adjustments to onboarding, with a richer introduction of our chatbot character, and a step that automatically enrolls users in the challenge that best fits their profile.

Launching Michael

After this round of usability improvements, our contract was over, and Michael: Cycling Companion went live on Messenger.

IMPACT

REFLECTING ON THE WORK

After launching, our biggest push for users resulted in a lackluster response. While disappointed, we were not overly surprised, as many users had expressed skepticism throughout our process.

I have many thoughts on where our team made good decisions, and where different actions may have increased our chance of success.

This project started as a product searching for a user base. I believe this particially clouded our team's decision-making throughout the project. For example, when faced with low user interest, our efforts to course-correct were often directed towards modifying the initial vision, rather than exploring new concepts which may have better addressed user needs.

In the future, I hope to give stronger pushback, and encourage more open-mindedness, whenever a client's product vision is particularly set in stone. As it relates to this project, considering platforms outside of chatbots and directions outside of broad, full-service motivation might have lead to a more successful solution.

PROBLEM SOLVING AT TOO BROAD A SCALE

I believe the scope of problems this project addressed was too broad. By focusing on full-service motivation, we were ignoring the details of users’ emotions and experiences within each step of the process. For example, Challenges were built on sound motivational tactics for cycling motivation as a whole, but failed to address micro-problems users face during the cycling educative process. As a result, our motivational triggers failed to be convincing.

Were I to start this project again, I would pick a problem identified in our research phase, such as bicycle purchasing or commute planning, and use more research methods to understand it deeply. In-person research such as ride-alongs, shop-alongs, repair-alongs, could have uncovered rich insights that lead to more focused, and potentially successful solution.

LENGTHY DEVELOPMENT AND TEST SPRINTS

Our team designed and iterated hundreds of pieces of educational content and chatbot conversations during our development process. Rather than create a few artifacts for usability testing, we often created many more to test usability and functionality. I think this categorically slowed down our learning process, leading to fewer insights that came later than would be ideal.
