MICHAEL: CYCLING COMPANION

In November 2017, I was contracted to design the UI and identity for an electric bicycle startup's new chatbot. Our challenge soon became to build a chatbot that educates and motivates adults to cycle more.

I spent 3 months as the designer and researcher for this new chatbot, in collaboration with one developer and one product manager.

Sections

  • Refinement
  • Research
  • Design
  • Impact

REFINEMENT

INITIAL CONTRACT

In fall 2017, Faraday Bicycles Inc. was exploring a new chatbot concept to integrate with their electric bicycles. I was contracted to build a prototype behind their vision of a “cycling companion.”

Requests

  • Create a compelling chatbot identity
  • Include a channel for customer support
  • Mockup data visualizations to display rider/bicycle activity
  • Brainstorm integration points between app and bicycle

Result

Faraday opted to pass the project, but one employee, Thomas Kreek, was enthusiastic enough to continue on his own. He offered me and a developer contracts to build-out a functional Beta for the chatbot.

EXTENDED CONTRACT

Thomas had new goals for the chatbot, which were ambitious, given our limited resources. I helped refine our focus by leading a "NUF" brainstorming session, and identifying success metrics.

Functional Goals

  • Educate: Teach non-cylists to become capable riders
  • Engage: Attract regular use from frequent cyclists
  • Mobilize: Create a brand for cycling evangelists to rally behind

Our "NUF" brainstorming revealed that all three goals would be hard to achieve. Despite this risk, my client decided to direct our work towards the Education goal of the product.

Defining success metrics

With this task at hand, I guided my client through brainstorming success metrics, to judge our progress.

  1. Ask Beta users to rate their confidence and motivation to cycle, before and after using our chatbot
  2. Focus on the percentage of adults who start cycling after using the chatbot, as an overall metric for success

RESEARCH

PLANNING

I advocated for upfront research to guide our product roadmap. My research plan included a literature review to understand broad pain points in cycling, and user interviews to fill-in the details of rider profiles and their preferences.

LITERATURE REVIEW

Goals

  • Narrow our target demographic
  • Find a problem area addressed by education/motivation
  • Inform a questionnaire to present in upcoming interviews

Narrowing user type

Many studies find that confidence, perceived benefits, and social support are the strongest positive indicators of cycling activity. With this in mind, I was inspired by a Portland-based study, which built a typology of cyclists based on rider confidence and enthusiasm.

"Strong and Fearless" and "No Way, No How" adults would be out of our scope, as they cycled regularly, or faced barriers we could not solve. Our focus would be on the two other groups (in blue, above).

Modeling behavioral change

Focusing on users' confidence and enthusiasm would allow us to design around a popular model of behavioral change, published by Stanford University’s Persuasive Technology Lab.

This model served as the foundation for our chatbot's educative process. Further research would identify aspects of Ability, Motivation, and Triggers most important to our target demographic.

Filtering out deterrents

In addition to boosting confidence and motivation, I hoped to find ways to problem-solve for our users. I began studying cycling deterrents, using affinity mapping create three categories. I also called out deterrents that seemed "unsolvable" by software.

Location

  • Hilly terrains
  • Dense traffic
  • Routes over 10km

Lifestyle

  • Lack of time
  • Low confidence*
  • Few opportunities*

Equipment

  • No bike*
  • Broken bike*
  • Safety equipment*

Many "solvable" deterrents had existing solutions, such as cycling education classes, and local bicycle shops. I hoped user interviews would uncover why our target demographic wasn't taking advantage of these solutions—perhaps multiple deterrents were confounding, or there were problems of accessibilty, cost, or quality.

USER INTERVIEWS

Goals

  • Understand relationships between common cycling deterrents
  • Identify tasks crucial to our demographic, but difficult to achieve

Asking open questions

We spoke with 16 adults across a range of demographics, cities, and cycling aptitudes. We discussed several topics, including past cycling experiences, and considerations when starting a cycling routine.

Topic 1: Location & Deterrents

"Have you biked in your current neighborhood? Why or why not?"
"When and where have you ever biked for transportation?"

Topic 2: Bicycles & Maintenance

"How has your bicycle’s condition kept you from cycling in the past?"
"How would you describe your local bicycle shop?"

Topic 3: Benefits

"How would cycling add or detract from your daily routine?"
What do you enjoy most about biking?

Identifying our niche audience

Many respondents mentioned unsolvable deterrents as their primary reason for not cycling, leading us to believe "No Way, No How" cyclists were larger than we thought.

The remaining users mentioned traffic fears, weather discomfort, and equipment maintainance moreso than unsolvable deterrents. We concluded our approach to confidence- and motivation-building would fit this niche audience.

CONSOLIDATING RESEARCH

The last step of my research process was drawing connections from findings to a psuedo-product roadmap.

Designing around tasks

I compiled a list of tasks respondents said were both crucial to cycling regularly, and difficult to achieve. These tasks would guide the confidence-building and motivating features I would prototype in our design phase.

Avoiding negative personas

I also constructed a few user personas. My goal was to keep our team aligned on who we were not building for, just as much as who we were building for. Pointing to names of individual proved very helpful in our upcoming design process.

DESIGN

DOCUMENTING CONSTRAINTS

Goals

  • Choose a development and distribution platform
  • Explore platform affordances and constraints before design

Comparing platform affordances

We opted to release on Facebook Messenger, after weighing the pros and cons of existing platforms and a native app. Bootstrapping on Messenger would be quicker than any alternative, and its ubiquity across devices, and unique chatbot affordances, were attractive.

Documenting constraints

We had a few explicit and generalized constraints to keep in mind after selecting Messenger as our platform:

  • Character limits and layout restrictions of card templates
  • Absence of traditional web or native app navigation options
  • Absence of traditional onboarding process to explain functionality
  • Brevity required for a chat-based, mobile UI

DESIGNING A CHARACTER

I was tasked to design a character behind our chatbot's content. The goal was to use engaging, character-driven copy to add emotional weight to our user interactions.

Profile and style guide

Keeping my original concept of Michael Faraday, I created a profile and style guide for writing copy. I described Michael as, "a curious scientist, and caring fitness coach, with high expectations."

1. Humanize Michael

  • Write in familiar language
  • Avoid monotony, excessive '!’s
  • Use facts, not platitudes
“Everyone can ride in the summer!”

"It is a perfect 70° today. I recommend cycling in a t-shirt.”

2. Build user confidence

  • Treat users with confidence
  • Ask users to one-up their best
  • Avoid vapid praise
“Great job! You are doing amazing.”

“You biked 5 miles today. How about 7 tomorrow?”

Card layouts

I designed a simple, flexible template for displaying a variety of information to users. The first applications of this template were Knowledge Cards, displaying bite-size information related to the challenging "Tasks" we identified in our research phase.

ITERATING ON CHATBOT UX

We built a simple two-level navigation system, where users could swipe through "Topic Cards" assigned to the parent topics of Knowledge Cards, before exploring a topic in detail.

Early usability testing revealed that non-cyclists felt intimidated when asked to explore cards by topic. I compiled feedback into two design problems:

  1. Lack of context

    Knowledge Cards alone were not substantial enough to create a lasting effect on users’ confidence.

    2. Quote:“This is hard for me to get through. To be honest, I’m not sure what I should be learning about.”

  2. Poor navigation

    Our navigation structure for Knowledge Cards did not match users’ mental models for learning

    3. Quote: “I can see how this would be helpful, but it’s too tedious for me to use it.”

Adding context around scenarios

We combined multiple Knowledge Cards, API calls, and questions to users to create guided procedures around common cycling scenarios.

Supporting Q&A interactions

We observed that most users began their chatbot interaction by asking a question. In attempt to fit users' mental model, we trained a NLP model to interpret user questions and direct them to the most relevant Knowledge Card.

GAMIFYING CYCLING

To increase user engagement, our team considered writing text-based, educational games around our existing features. The vision was to provide users with end-to-end guidance along real-world goals, e.g. learning to cycle in traffic, or commuting to work.

Goal

  • Use small, consecutive recommendations to guide users through long-term tasks
  • Increase user motivation by adding process-oriented goals to existing features
  • Add fun, engaging interactions with users' local environment through API calls

I distributed SurveyMonkey survey asking users to provide feedback on our initial game ideas. We received a ranking of ideas, but most users were underwhelmed by the concept overall.

  1. Confident riders were uninterested

    2. Quote:"I'm very happy already with Strava, where I can measure myself and set my own goals. I don't need someone else inventing arbitrary tasks for me."

  2. Inexperienced riders were barely interested

    Under half of inexperienced riders said were interested in changing their cycling behavior, and even fewer said they would participate in challenges orchestrated by an app

Despite the dreary feedback, my client asked us to continue to develop three cycling games in the last two months of our contract.

Designing around user goals

I wrote game logic and copy for each game. Each game's checkpoints were aimed at user goals and important tasks we identified during our research phase. Our games included:

  • Zero to One: Transitioning from non-cyclist to regular cyclist
  • Marco Polo: Setting goals to bike to local attractions
  • Workplace Warrior: Setting goals for bike-to-work commutes

Early usability testing revealed that "less text," "more visual content," and "explanation of in-game progress" were helpful to keep users engaged over time.

OBSERVING LOW ENGAGEMENT

In our last two weeks, we made a big push for more users, but received a lackluster response from our cycling games. While disappointed, we were not overly surprised, based on the results of our latest survey, and plenty of skepticism we received throughout the development process.

Michael: Cycling Companion is now live on Facebook Messenger, and I check-in regularly with my client to see how user engagement fluctuates. Ultimately, the product did not succeed at its goal of the motivating a high percentage of reluctant cyclists to start cycling. My client is now in contact with cycling manufacturers, and advocacy groups, where he (and I) hope its functionality may serve useful.

ASSESSING IMPACT

REFLECTING ON THE WORK

As I reached the end of my six month contract, I had many ideas where both I and our team could have made different decision to benefit the outcome of the project.

STARTING WITH A PRODUCT IN MIND

This project started as a product searching for a user base. I believe this particially clouded our team's decision-making throughout the project. For example, when faced with low user interest, our efforts to course-correct were often directed towards modifying the initial vision, rather than exploring new concepts which may have better addressed user needs.

In the future, I hope to give stronger pushback, and encourage more open-mindedness, whenever a client's product vision is particularly set in stone. As it relates to this project, considering platforms outside of chatbots and directions outside of broad, full-service motivation might have lead to a more successful solution.

PROBLEM SOLVING AT TOO BROAD A SCALE

I believe the scope of problems this project addressed was too broad. By focusing on full-service motivation, we were ignoring the details of users’ emotions and experiences within each step of the process. For example, Challenges were built on sound motivational tactics for cycling motivation as a whole, but failed to address micro-problems users face during the cycling educative process. As a result, our motivational triggers failed to be convincing.

Were I to start this project again, I would pick a problem identified in our research phase, such as bicycle purchasing or commute planning, and use more research methods to understand it deeply. In-person research such as ride-alongs, shop-alongs, repair-alongs, could have uncovered rich insights that lead to more focused, and potentially successful solution.

LENGTHY DEVELOPMENT AND TEST SPRINTS

Our team designed and iterated hundreds of pieces of educational content and chatbot conversations during our development process. Rather than create a few artifacts for usability testing, we often created many more to test usability and functionality. I think this categorically slowed down our learning process, leading to fewer insights that came later than would be ideal.
