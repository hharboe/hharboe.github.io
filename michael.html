Michael: Cycling Assistant

In November 2017, an electric bicycle startup hired me to design the interface and identity for a new chatbot concept.

One month later, I was contracted to design a Beta version. I led research and design for 3 months, and collaborated with one developer and our client/product manager.

Sections

  • Scope
  • Research
  • Design
  • Impact

SCOPE

FIRST CONTRACT

In fall 2017, Faraday Bicycles Inc. wanted to build a chatbot to integrate with their electric bicycles. I was contracted for two weeks to refine their concept of a “cycling companion chatbot," and design an accompanying visual prototype.

Requests

  • Design a character that's entertaining and brand-aligned
  • Brainstorm hardware/software integration points
  • Mockup interface components, including a customer support channel, and activity data visualizations

Results

Faraday opted to pass the project, but one employee, Thomas Kreek, decided to continue on his own. He offered me and a developer contracts to build a functional Beta version.

SECOND CONTRACT

Refining goals

Our second contract's functional goals felt ambitious, given our resources. In the first meeting, I led a "NUF" brainstorm to help refine our focus and define success metrics.

  • Educate: Teach non-cylists to become capable riders
  • Engage: Attract regular use from frequent cyclists
  • Mobilize: Create a brand for cycling evangelists to rally behind

Defining success metrics

Despite the risk shown by our "NUF" brainstorm, my client decided to focus on the Education direction, hoping to convince new and irregular cyclists to adopt new cycling routines.

We would ask how many days/week users cycled, at and up to 3 months after sign up, as a proxy for a users' cycling routine durability.

RESEARCH

RESEARCH PLANNING

My generative research plan included a literature review to identify cycling pain points, and user interviews to understand rider types.

LITERATURE REVIEW

Objectives

  • Identify cycling problems in scope
  • Narrow target demographic
  • Prepare questionnaire for upcoming interviews

Identifying cycling problems

I searched existing literature for problems facing new and irregular cyclists, and affinity mapped categories across multiple studies. I crossed-out problems I felt were unsolvable by software.

Location

  • Hilly terrains
  • Dense traffic
  • Routes over 10km

Lifestyle

  • Lack of time
  • Low confidence*
  • Few opportunities*

Equipment

  • No bike*
  • Broken bike*
  • Safety equipment*

We learned that the most prevalent problems were unsolvable, or out of our scope, and many solvable problems had existing solutions. For example, cities often have classes to teach riding skills and bicycle shops to fix equipment.

I hoped to uncover why new and irregular cyclists didn't utilize existing solutions—perhaps they faced confounding deterrents, or their solutions were inaccessible.

Understanding rider types

Many studies categorize cyclists based on psychological qualities. One convincing Portland-based study built a typology of cyclists based on rider confidence and enthusiasm.

Using their study as an analogy for our target users, we decided to focus on the groups in blue, who lack the confidence or enthusiasm to adopt a regular cycling routine.

Applying a behavioral change model

With this focus on boosting confidence and enthusiasm, we found inspiration in a behavioral change model popularized by Stanford’s Persuasive Technology Lab.

This simple model became the foundation for our chatbot's educative process. We hoped our interviews would identify the elements of Ability, Motivation, and Triggers prioritized by our target users.

USER INTERVIEWS

Objectives

  • Understand interactions between solvable/unsolvable problems
  • Identify crucial tasks in journeys to develop cycling routines
  • Distinguish behavioral differences between rider types

Asking open questions

We spoke with 16 adults across a range of demographics, cities, and cycling aptitudes. Our discussion addressed past cycling experiences, and habits related to maintaining cycling routines.

Topic 1: Location

"Have you biked in your current neighborhood? Why or why not?"
"When and where have you ever biked for transportation?"

Topic 2: Lifestyle

"How would cycling add or detract from your daily routine?"
"What do you enjoy most about biking?"

Topic 3: Equipment

"How has your bicycle’s condition kept you from cycling in the past?"
"How would you describe your local bicycle shop?"

Identifying a niche audience

Our interviews showed that our target proportion of adults (those who face only solvable deterrents) was smaller than we expected. The most common deterrents they faced included traffic fears, weather discomfort, and bike maintainance.

Avoiding negative personas

After interviews, I constructed user personas to align our team on who and who not to design for. Pointing to names and faces was very helpful when discussing design decisions.

Designing around tasks

I also categorized all tasks our respondents described as crucial to maintain cycling routines. We would later design our product roadmap around making these tasks more approachable.

DESIGN

PICKING A PLATFORM

Goals

  • Pick our chatbot's development and distribution platform
  • Identify chosen platform's affordances and constraints

Comparing platform affordances

After considering using an existing chatbot platform, or building a native app, we decided to release on Facebook Messenger. Messenger would allow us to boostrap the quickest, and its popularity and unique affordances were attractive from a design perspective.

Documenting constraints

Early on, I documented the constraints we faced by using Messenger's conversational features and UI:

  • Character limits and layout restrictions of card templates
  • Absence of traditional navigation affordances
  • Reliance on conversational experience in user onboarding
  • Brevity required for a chat-based, mobile UI

CHARACTER DESIGN

Objectives

  • Create guidelines for writing cohesive, character-driven copy
  • Design flexible templates for all visual content

Creating a personality

My client wanted to add emotional weight to our user interactions with meaningful and authentic copy, written from our character's perspective. I described Michael Faraday as, "a curious scientist, and caring fitness coach, with high expectations," and wrote guidelines for how our team should write copy.

1. Humanize Michael

  • Write in familiar language
  • Avoid monotony, excessive '!’s
  • Use facts, not platitudes
“Everyone can ride in the summer!”
"It is a perfect 70° today. I recommend cycling in a t-shirt.”

2. Build user confidence

  • Treat users with confidence
  • Ask users to one-up their best
  • Avoid vapid praise
“Great job! You are doing amazing.”
“You biked 5 miles today. How about 7 tomorrow?”

Optimizing for mobile

To display visual information to users, I designed a simple, flexible card template. The first applications of this template were Knowledge Cards, which contained bite-size information related to tasks identified in our research.

ITERATING ON UI

Objectives

  • Minimize friction of conversational navigation affordances
  • Fit information architecture to users' mental models
  • Prototype and test for unknown frictions in chatbot interface

Documenting frictions

We built a two-level navigation system, where users could swipe through parent topics, before exploring detailed Knowledge Cards.

Early usability testing showed non-cyclists feeling intimidated to explore cards by topic. I compiled feedback into two problems:

1. Lack of Substance

  • Knowledge Cards alone did not create a lasting effect on users’ confidence.
“This is hard for me. To be honest, I’m not sure what to learn about.”

2. Poor navigation

  • Our navigation structure did not match users’ mental models for learning
“I can see this being helpful, but I think it’s too tedious for me."

Matching real-life scenarios

To give users more context, we combined multiple Knowledge Cards and API calls to create guidance around common cycling scenarios.

Supporting Q&A interactions

We observed most users starting their chatbot interaction by asking a question. To fit these users' mental models, we trained a NLP model to interpret user questions and direct them to the most relevant card.

PIVOTING OUR SOLUTION

Problem

During usability testing, users were questioning the desirability of our features. Our cycling guide was called "uninteresting" and "incomplete." When asked if our chatbot was empowering towards fitness or commute goals, intermediate cyclists said "not at all."

Solution

Our team decided to "gamify" our chatbot, through conversation-based games. This meant incorporating our existing features into activity- or education-based challenges related to cycling.

Gauging interest with surveys

I distributed a SurveyMonkey survey asking for feedback on our game ideas. About 25% of inexperienced riders had interest in cycling challenges orchestrated by an app, and experienced riders were not at all interested. One experienced rider said, "I'm very happy already with Strava, where I can measure myself and set my own goals. I don't need someone else inventing arbitrary tasks for me."

A broader, more disappointing piece of feedback was that less than 50% of inexperienced riders said were interested in changing their cycling behavior at all. Despite this negative response, my client asked us to continue to develop three cycling games in the last two months of our contract.

Despite the dreary feedback, my client asked us to continue to develop three cycling games in the last two months of our contract.

Designing around user goals

I wrote game logic and copy for each game. Each game's checkpoints were aimed at user goals and important tasks we identified during our research phase. Our games included:

  • Zero to One: Transitioning from non-cyclist to regular cyclist
  • Marco Polo: Setting goals to bike to local attractions
  • Workplace Warrior: Setting goals for bike-to-work commutes

Early usability testing revealed that "less text," "more visual content," and "explanation of in-game progress" were helpful to keep users engaged over time. To address this, I incorporated more frequent checkpoints, more emojis to break-up walls of text, and more image-based cards for richer information.

OBSERVING LOW ENGAGEMENT

In our last two weeks, we made a big push for more users, but received a lackluster response from our cycling games. While disappointed, we were not overly surprised, based on the results of our latest survey, and plenty of skepticism we received throughout the development process.

Michael: Cycling Companion is now live on Facebook Messenger, and I check-in regularly with my client to see how user engagement fluctuates. Ultimately, the product did not succeed at its goal of the motivating a high percentage of reluctant cyclists to start cycling. My client is now in contact with cycling manufacturers, and advocacy groups, where he (and I) hope its functionality may serve useful.

ASSESSING IMPACT

REFLECTING ON THE WORK

As I reached the end of my six month contract, I had many ideas where both I and our team could have made different decision to benefit the outcome of the project.

STARTING WITH A PRODUCT IN MIND

This project started as a product searching for a user base. I believe this particially clouded our team's decision-making throughout the project. For example, when faced with low user interest, our efforts to course-correct were often directed towards modifying the initial vision, rather than exploring new concepts which may have better addressed user needs.

In the future, I hope to give stronger pushback, and encourage more open-mindedness, whenever a client's product vision is particularly set in stone. As it relates to this project, considering platforms outside of chatbots and directions outside of broad, full-service motivation might have lead to a more successful solution.

PROBLEM SOLVING AT TOO BROAD A SCALE

I believe the scope of problems this project addressed was too broad. By focusing on full-service motivation, we were ignoring the details of users’ emotions and experiences within each step of the process. For example, Challenges were built on sound motivational tactics for cycling motivation as a whole, but failed to address micro-problems users face during the cycling educative process. As a result, our motivational triggers failed to be convincing.

Were I to start this project again, I would pick a problem identified in our research phase, such as bicycle purchasing or commute planning, and use more research methods to understand it deeply. In-person research such as ride-alongs, shop-alongs, repair-alongs, could have uncovered rich insights that lead to more focused, and potentially successful solution.

LENGTHY DEVELOPMENT AND TEST SPRINTS

Our team designed and iterated hundreds of pieces of educational content and chatbot conversations during our development process. Rather than create a few artifacts for usability testing, we often created many more to test usability and functionality. I think this categorically slowed down our learning process, leading to fewer insights that came later than would be ideal.
