Michael: Cycling Assistant

In late 2017, Faraday Bicycles was searching for new way to share the value of electric bicycles with their target market. They decided to build the first "cycling chatbot"—a tool for accessing practical cycling information, activity data, and local recommendations.

My Role

I was contracted to design a functional Beta around this idea. I collaborated with one freelance developer, and one Faraday manager, who led us through a four month development process.

My responsibilities included:

  • Helping define the project's scope
  • Leading generative research
  • Leading feature design and copywriting
  • Leading usability tests and revisions

Sections

  • Scoping
  • Discovery
  • Design
  • Impact

SCOPING

CONCEPT PROTOTYPING

My first task was to design a visual prototype around my client's vision for a "cycling companion chatbot."

Requests

  • Create an entertaining, brand-aligned character for the chatbot
  • Show examples of activity data visualizations in conversations

Results

After approving the project, Faraday decided to pivot away from integrating with electric bikes, and focus on building an information hub for all types of cyclists.

TRIMMING SCOPE

Faraday had three objectives for their chatbot:

  • Motivate non-cyclists to start biking
  • Engage infrequent cyclists to ride more
  • Help cycling evangelist groups rally their members

Pursuing all three goals felt ambitious for our small team, so I recommended we pick one North Star objective to pursue deeply.

Weighing complexity

In our first client meeting, I led a "NUF" brainstorm to evaluate the merits and complexity of each idea.

The "NUF" analysis clarified that each goal would be challenging to achieve. Succeeding at all three would be far beyond our budget.

Expanding on opportunities

After agreeing to limit our scope, our team began developing an Opportunity-Solution Tree to identify which objective had the most promising opportunities.

We individually brainstormed opportunities and solutions under each objective, and came together to evaluate our ideas.

Motivating reluctant cyclists was the clear favorite. Our next task would be to conduct a brief literature review, and user interviews, to discover which opportunity to design for.

DISCOVERY

REVIEWING LITERATURE

In preparation for user interviews, I spent some time looking for existing studies on cyclists' behavior, goals, and challenges.

Differentiating users

Few studies prioritize cyclist goals over demographics, but one Portland-based study found a strong correlation between goals and two psychological factors: cycling confidence and enthusiasm.

In our upcoming user interviews, we hoped this framework would provide a helpful starting point to segmenting users by goals.

Previewing a solution

Before moving on, it felt relevant for our team to discuss BJ Fogg's behavioral change model, which emphasizes the importance of ability and motivation (similar to confidence and enthusiasm).

We felt this model could inspire how our chatbot motivated users. We hoped interviews might identify factors of confidence/ability, enthusiasm/motivation, and triggers prioritized by our audience.

INTERVIEWING CYCLISTS

We spoke with 9 adults in our target segment, and 7 in the No Way, No How or Strong and Fearless segments. We asked open-ended questions about past experiences and current behaviors related to cycling and commuting.

Who we spoke with:

Exploring user goals

Hard to inspire non-interested commuters to adopt cycling

"Excessive time spent" was the most common frustration we heard related to commuting by car and public transit. Mentions of "discomfort" and "lack of physical activity" were in the minority, with almost no mention of financial or environmental costs.

sa

"Lack of opportunities" was the most common deterrent we heard for cycling, with "fear of traffic" and "difficulty/discomfort" closely following for users with low self-rated confidence.

Our challenge was that cycling would not solve the issues of "time spent" or "discomfort" for most users. "Lack of opportunities" and "fear of traffic" were mostly out of the range of our impact, aside from recommendations or tips.

We couldn't solve problems for users with low enthusiasm, and we could only partially solve problems or users with low confidence/high enthusiasm.

The problem space was not focused on one thing. Amalgam of confounding issues. We could either try to address one, try to focus on a few, or try to switch to a different opportunity. I left my client to decide.

We decided to focus on a few, with an emphasis on "fun" to make the app engaging despite a lack of problem-solution match.

I constructed user personas to align our team on who and who not to design for. Having characters to point to was very helpful during the design process.

Focusing on crucial tasks

We used interviews to identify and categorize tasks that are crucial to developing cycling routines. The goal of our chatbot was to make these tasks more approachable for users.

DESIGN

CHOOSING TECHNOLOGY

Goals

  • Choose our chatbot's development and distribution platform
  • Identify chosen platform's affordances and constraints

Comparing affordances

We bootstrapped on Facebook Messenger, rather than other chatbot platforms, or a native app. Messenger was popular across devices, had useful chat affordances, and made development the quickest.

Documenting constraints

Before designing, I documented Messenger's chatbot UI constraints:

  • Character limits and layout restrictions of card templates
  • Absence of traditional navigation affordances
  • Brevity required for a chat-based, mobile UI

CHARACTER DESIGN

Objectives

  • Create guidelines for writing cohesive, character-driven copy
  • Design flexible templates for all visual content

Creating a personality

My client wanted to add emotional weight to our user interactions with meaningful, character-driven copy. I wrote copywriting guidelines, describing Michael as, "a curious scientist, and caring fitness coach, with high expectations."

1. Humanize Michael

  • Write in familiar language
  • Avoid monotony, excessive '!’s
  • Use facts, not platitudes
“Everyone can ride in the summer!”
"It is a perfect 70° today. I recommend cycling in a t-shirt.”

2. Build user confidence

  • Treat users with confidence
  • Ask users to one-up their best
  • Avoid vapid praise
“Great job! You are doing amazing.”
“You biked 5 miles today. How about 7 tomorrow?”

Optimizing for mobile

To display visual information, I designed a simple, flexible card template. Knowledge Cards were the first template application, using bite-size information to guide users through crucial cycling tasks.

IMPROVING USABILITY

Objectives

  • Minimize friction of conversation-based navigation
  • Fit information architecture to users' mental models
  • Prototype and test for unknown frictions in chatbot interface

Documenting frictions

We built a two-level navigation system, where users could swipe through parent topics before exploring detailed Knowledge Cards.

Early usability testing showed non-cyclists feeling intimidated to explore cards by topic. I compiled feedback into two problems:

1. Lack of Substance

  • Knowledge Cards alone did not improve users’ confidence.
“This is hard for me. To be honest, I’m not sure what to learn about.”

2. Poor navigation

  • Our navigation structure did not match users’ mental models
“I can see this being helpful, but I think it’s too tedious for me."

Matching real-life scenarios

To give users more context, we combined multiple Knowledge Cards and API calls to create guidance around common cycling scenarios.

Supporting Q&A interactions

We observed most users starting their chatbot interaction by asking a question. To fit these users' mental models, we trained a NLP model to interpret user questions and direct them to the most relevant card.

GAMIFYING FEATURES

Throughout testing, users questioned the helpfulness of our features, calling our cycling guide "uninteresting" or "incomplete." Others said they felt not at all empowered to pursue new habits.

Our team decided to gamify our chatbot, by adding process-oriented motivation into existing features. New activity- or education-based challenges could be progressed-through via chatbot interactions.

Collecting feedback

25% of inexperienced riders, and 0% of experienced riders, expressed interest in our challenges idea. One rider said, "I'm happy with Strava, where I set my own goals. I don't need more arbitrary tasks."

Despite this negative response, my client asked us to continue to develop three cycling games in the last two months of our contract.

Designing around user goals

I wrote each challenge's logic and copy around steps to achieve cycling-related goals. Our challenges included:

  • Zero to One: Transitioning from non-cyclist to regular cyclist
  • Marco Polo: Biking to more local destinations
  • Workplace Warrior: Starting a bike-to-work commute routine

Improving usability

Usability testing revealed user frustrations with "walls of text," and a lack of progress indicators within challenges. My solution was to add frequent checkpoints, emojis, and images, for richer displays.

We also updated our onboarding, with a playful introduction to our character, and a step to enroll users in their most relevant challenge.

Launching Michael

After this round of usability improvements, our contract was over, and Michael: Cycling Companion went live on Messenger.

IMPACT

LOW ENGAGEMENT

After launching, our biggest push for users resulted in a lackluster response. While disappointed, we were not overly surprised, as many users had expressed skepticism throughout our process.

I still check-in regularly with my client to hear how engagement is fluctuating, and if there are any updates planned. My client is now contacting bicycle manufacturers and advocacy groups, where he hopes our work can add value.

POST-WORK REFLECTIONS

I have many thoughts on where our team made good decisions, and where different actions may have increased our chance of success.

Insights on product design

  1. A product spec without a use case leads to inefficient design

    • Pressure to satisfy the initial vision distracts the design process from course-correcting
    • Too many initial constraints stifle opportunities to experiment

  2. Motivational content, on its own, results in low engagement

    • Motivational content engages users less than content that is entertaining, or solves a user's problem
    • A motivational service must also address hurdles facing users, before expecting a change in behavior

  3. Solutions aimed at too broad a problem will overlook critical opportunities to add value

    • Solutions designed for broad problems may fail to address the most crucial hurdles in users' experiences
    • Addressing many problems at once results many poor solutions
    • Expanding on one proven solution is simpler than searching for value in a broad, unproven solution

Insights on chatbots

  1. Chatbots work best in short-form, functional interactions

    • Reading and responding to a chatbot increases time spent engaging with content, and limits opportunities to skim content
    • A conversational approach to content engagement may lead to better retention, or interruptions in comprehension (unclear)

  2. Authoring a humanoid chatbot is best done algorithmically

    • Human interactions are character-driven, empathetic, highly variable, and hard to imitate
    • Imitating human conversations is tedious for chatbot authors, and believability, as a target, is abstract
    • A lack of writing time leads most chatbots to include drab, hyper-functional, and uncanny language
    • Scaling copywriting for a humanoid chatbot likely requires an automated, algorithmic approach

Future recommendations to self

  • Give more pushback when the product spec lacks a use case
  • Shorten prototype-and-test cycles (we should have aimed for 2 week cycles, not 3, and never 4) to find the most impactful problem/solution more quickly
  • Grade usability tests on a rubric, tracking variables over time
  • Reduce scope of target problem. For example, instead of focusing on “educating non-cyclists,” focus on a tangible problem, like "most adults find routine bike maintenance inconvenient”
  • Spend more time understanding a single problem with high-touch methods. For example, to research cycling maintenance, go on ride-alongs, repair-alongs, and shop-alongs
