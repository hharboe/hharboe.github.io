LUMENED: EDUCATION STARTUP

LumenEd was the edtech startup I co-founded my junior year of college, and worked on full-time after graduation. Over three years, our team designed and shipped a hardware, software, and curriculum products to K-8 classrooms.

I was responsible for curriculum and software design, marketing materials, and sales to K-8 schools. My two co-founders lead software development, fundraising, and hardware development.

RESEARCH

INITIAL VISION

LumenEd's inspiration came from appreciation for the growing culture of video-based education in the US, and concern over its inaccessibility in underprivileged parts of the world.

Using the latest projector, video camera, and micro-PC technology, we saw an opportunity to design a portable, "smart projector” for classrooms. We thought this concept would compete on price and classroom infrastructure requirements, compared to other classroom technology alternatives.

PILOTS & FIELD TESTS

We tested the concept in summer 2013 with a retail projector preloaded with educational videos. We brought the projector to underresourced classrooms in New Delhi, and asked for feedback.

Both teachers and students were enthusiastic about the concept. With motivation to test a custom smart projector the following summer, we observed and documented technical requirements for our hardware.

THREE PRODUCT DIRECTIONS

With $10k from social impact grants and a pitch competition, we set out to test two applications of our prototype: playing video content, and facilitating a video exchange program between classrooms. Field testing led to several conclusions:

HARDWARE IS EFFECTIVE

Spring through summer 2014 was devoted to design, assembly, and shipment of our BOB prototypes. We ran into numerous headaches throughout this process, teaching us that pursuing BOBs in the future would be best accomplished through a professional manufacturer.

CONTENT IS NOT A PRIORITY

To explore how much teachers appreciated video content coming pre-loaded on the BOB, we curated and shipped a library of open-source videos with our prototypes. We ultimately learned that teachers prefer to find and use their own materials.

CLASSROOM PARTNERSHIPS ARE LOVED

We found three schools in the US willing to participate in a video exchange project with our Indian school partners over the summer. With support from our team, these classrooms sent numerous video diaries to each other over three months, covering their lives at school, their local communities, and favorite cultural practices.

Students on both sides of the exchange had wonderful experiences filming videos in their classrooms, and seeing videos from their new friends abroad. The biggest takeaways were that Indian classrooms needed hardware to participate, and US classrooms were enthusiastic enough to pay for the experience again.

EXPLORING 'GLOBAL EDUCATION'

Following the success of our video exchanges, we wanted to learn more about ‘global education,’ a growing trend in American K-8 schools curricula. One common approach we found was DIY classroom projects organized by proactive, globally-minded teachers.

We spoke with two organizations and one startup assisting teachers with these projects, and asked what factors they felt were most crucial to success. We coupled their responses with feedback from our pilot program teachers to create a list of requirements for our own program.

DESIGN

CRAFTING A UNIQUE PROGRAM

We used our research findings to guide decisions as we fleshed out the details of our video exchange concept, now called Video Pen Pals. To communicate our offering to schools, we launched a website, with mockups of curricula and software products.

MULTIPLE LEARNING APPROACHES

We put our program's unique, multi-layered pedalogical approach at the front of our marketing materials. This included two distinct topic areas designed for a younger and older group of students.

CONFIDENCE-BUILDING VIDEO SOFTWARE

Our video software highlighted simple recording and editing. Our messaging and video exchange portal emphasized that the burden of managing logistics fell to LumenEd, and that support was always a quick message away.

LOW-COMMITMENT EARLY ENROLLMENT

Our website used a low-commitment call-to-action, asking teachers to reserve a spot rather than enroll early. This allowed us to start dialogs and learn from our first customers, rather than isolate them with a schedule or price that didn't fit.

INCORPORATING THE 'BOB'

As an organization, we saw a path forward that dropped the Bright Orange Box and focused on selling Video Pen Pals to well-off schools already equipped with technology. While this would be a low-risk business direction, it would be a partial abandonment of our founding mission and traction using the BOB to impact offline, disconnected classrooms.

We decided to pursue a dual-focused path for the upcoming year: earn revenue from schools through Video Pen Pals, and invest funds into BOB manufacturing. If we were successful, distributing BOBs to a diverse set of international NGOs would eventually yield its own revenue, and simultaneously expand the reach of Video Pen Pals.

GROWTH

FUNDRAISING CHALLENGES

After deciding to pursue LumenEd full-time after school, we set a goal to raise $200k by the day we graduated. These funds would support a year of operations and a manufacturing run of 250 Bright Orange Boxes. Our primary fundraising sources were classroom pre-sales and a Kickstarter campaign. We also began sending applications to social impact grants and looking to network with angel investors.

PRE-SALES ARE RARE IN EDTECH

Pre-sales were our preferred fundraising method, as we knew early sales traction would make us more attractive to investors. We tested this approach in March with our pilot schools, offering a 25% discount and special perks in exchange for an early commitment to the fall. While every pilot school was interested in the opportunity, not one was able to commit funds so early.
